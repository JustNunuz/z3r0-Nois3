The rapid advancements in artificial intelligence and natural language processing have brought significant benefits to global communication. However, a major challenge remains in how these systems handle diverse linguistic structures. Most modern large language models rely on tokenization methods optimized primarily for English and other linguistically similar languages. This creates a disparity when these models attempt to process highly agglutinative languages such as Shona, where a single word might convey the meaning of an entire sentence in English. As a result, the models split these rich linguistic constructs into an excessive number of meaningless sub-word fragments, a phenomenon we refer to as deep fragmentation. Ultimately, this inefficiency not only drives up computational costs but also degrades the model's semantic comprehension, highlighting the need for more culturally aware and structurally adaptive architectures.
